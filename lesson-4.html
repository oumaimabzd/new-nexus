Lesson 4: Converging Threats

When we think about threats to schools, we tend to think in categories. Extremism is one problem. Gangs are another. School attacks are something else entirely. And the internet — well, that's an IT issue.

This way of thinking is understandable. It's how threats are typically presented in the media, in policy documents, and in most training programmes. But it is also dangerously incomplete.

The reality is that these threats do not exist in isolation. They overlap. They feed each other. And increasingly, they operate through the same channels, target the same young people, and exploit the same vulnerabilities.

Sweden is experiencing a documented rise in gang-related violence, with an increasing number of minors involved in serious crimes including shootings and bombings. At the same time, extremism — whether Islamist, far-right, or far-left — is growing, and the growth of one form often fuels the growth of another. These are not parallel developments. Criminal gangs in Sweden are frequently driven by extremist ideologies, while extremist organisations exploit gang networks to carry out assassinations, spread disinformation, organise unrest, and destabilise society.

Targeted school violence sits at the centre of this convergence. The majority of large-scale school attacks — both in Sweden and internationally — have been carried out by individuals, often minors, driven by extremist ideologies. These individuals did not radicalise in a vacuum. They radicalised online.

And that is the critical connecting factor. The internet and social media are where gangs recruit children. Where extremist groups radicalise them. Where future attackers consume violent content, express escalating views, publish manifestos, announce their intentions — and in some cases, livestream their attacks to the world.

This is not a collection of separate problems that happen to coexist. It is an integrated threat system. Each element reinforces the others, and the digital sphere acts as the accelerant that binds them together.

For schools, this means that a student showing signs of gang involvement may also be undergoing radicalisation. A student consuming extremist content online may be on a trajectory toward targeted violence. And the warning signs — withdrawal, behavioural changes, exposure to harmful online content — often look the same regardless of which specific threat is emerging.

If you only understand one piece of this picture, you will miss connections that could have been acted on. And in school security, missed connections cost lives.

The Swedish government has recognised this reality. Extremism, gang crime, and school violence have all been elevated to national security priorities, and for the first time, schools have been explicitly identified as frontline actors in the prevention of these threats. This is not a role schools chose. But it is a role schools now carry.

Understanding the converging nature of these threats is not academic. It is the foundation for everything that follows in this course — from recognising early warning signs, to knowing when and how to report, to building a school culture where prevention is embedded in everyday practice.

You cannot prevent what you do not understand. And you cannot understand these threats by looking at them one at a time.

Fact Sheet

Converging Threats: Extremism, Gangs, Targeted Violence, and the Digital Sphere

Introduction: Schools are vulnerable to various types and levels of risk. This fact sheet focuses on four central risk areas with significant impact on schools and society: criminal gangs, extremism, school attacks, and risks linked to the internet and social media.
Important: Although these risks are often defined and managed separately, they are in reality interconnected and mutually influential. They can even reinforce each other, blur the boundaries between them, and intensify the overall effect.
1

Criminal Gangs

National Concern

Criminal gangs have become a major concern for the Swedish government and security services due to the level of fatal violence in the country.

Involvement of Minors

Particularly concerning is the involvement of minors in these activities, which places Sweden among the countries in Europe with the highest rates of shootings and bombings.

Active Recruitment of Children: Criminal gangs actively attempt to recruit children and minors for their criminal purposes.
2

Growing Extremism

Multi-Faceted Growth

Sweden is seeing a marked increase not only in one, but in multiple forms of extremism — Islamist, far-right, and far-left.

Mutual Reinforcement

Notably, the growth of one form of extremism can often fuel the growth of another, particularly those perceived as each other's polar opposites.

Recruitment of Children: Like gangs, extremist groups actively attempt to recruit children.

The Connection: Gangs and Extremism

Gangs in Sweden are not infrequently driven by extremist ideologies and use radical interpretations of religion and ideology to further their criminal purposes.

Furthermore, extremist regimes and organisations often exploit gangs for:

  • Contract killings — often carried out by children and minors
  • Spreading disinformation
  • Organising riots and demonstrations
  • Attempting to destabilise the country at multiple levels
3

School Attacks and Targeted Violence

Key Concern for the Education Sector

School attacks are naturally a key concern for the education sector and represent one of the most feared threats to student safety.

Extremism as a Driving Force

The majority of school shootings and large-scale deadly attacks on schools, both globally and in Sweden, have been carried out by individuals — often minors — driven by extremist ideologies.

4

The Digital Sphere: The Critical Connecting Factor

The internet and social media are the critical connecting factor between all these risks.

Strategic Exploitation of Platforms:

  • Criminal gangs and extremist groups exploit these platforms strategically for communication, planning, and coordination of activities
  • Radicalisation and recruitment of children occurs actively through social media

Connection to School Attacks:

  • A significant proportion of school attacks have been preceded by online radicalisation
  • Perpetrators have expressed extreme views online over extended periods
  • Many have announced their plans and published manifestos online
  • Tragically, many perpetrators have also livestreamed their attacks on social platforms

Summary: An Integrated Threat System

These four risk areas — gang criminality, extremism, school violence, and digital threats — do not constitute isolated phenomena. They are deeply interconnected and reinforce each other:

  • Gangs are driven by extremist ideologies
  • Extremist organisations exploit gangs
  • School attacks are often driven by extremist radicalisation
  • The internet and social media enable and amplify all these threats
  • Children and minors are particularly vulnerable to recruitment and radicalisation
National Priority: Because these areas — particularly extremism, gang criminality, and school violence — have been identified as priorities by the Swedish government and are closely linked to national security, they require detailed understanding of the historical, societal, and security context, as well as in-depth analysis of how they affect society at large and schools in particular.

Reflection

Take a moment to reflect on what you have learned and how it applies to your professional context.

Consideration

Think about the statement: "You cannot prevent what you do not understand. And you cannot understand these threats by looking at them one at a time." What does this mean for how your school currently approaches threat awareness — and are you looking at the full picture, or only the parts that feel most familiar?

Think about which of the four threat categories — extremism, gang crime, targeted school violence, or digital risks — your school discusses the least. Consider what that silence might be costing you.

Self-Reflection Questions

1

Before this lesson, did you think of extremism, gang crime, school attacks, and online risks as separate issues — or as interconnected? How has your understanding changed, and what does that shift mean for how you observe and interpret student behaviour?

2

Can you think of a student — past or present — who showed signs that could have been linked to more than one threat category? For example, a student involved in online extremist communities who was also socially isolated, or a student with gang connections who was showing signs of radicalisation. Was that connection recognised at the time?

3

Sweden has been identified as one of the countries in Europe with the highest rates of shootings and bombings, with minors increasingly involved. How aware are students and staff at your school of this reality — and is it something that is openly discussed, or treated as something that happens elsewhere?

4

Extremist groups and criminal gangs both actively recruit children and young people. How confident are you that staff at your school could distinguish between normal adolescent behaviour and the early signs of recruitment or radicalisation? Rate your confidence on a scale of 1-10 — and identify what would need to change to raise that number.

5

The lesson identified the digital sphere as the critical connecting factor between all four threat categories. Is your school actively engaged in understanding what students encounter online — or is the internet treated as something outside the school's domain? What are the consequences of that position?

6

The growth of one form of extremism often fuels the growth of another. Have you observed this dynamic in your local community or in conversations among students? How equipped is your school to address extremism in all its forms — not just the one that receives the most media attention?

7

Think about the concept of an integrated threat system — where gangs, extremism, school violence, and digital risks reinforce each other. Does your school's current security planning reflect this reality, or does it address each risk in isolation? What would an integrated approach look like in practice at your school?

8

Name three specific actions you will take within the next month to strengthen your ability — and your school's ability — to recognise and respond to converging threats. Be concrete: who will you speak to, what will you read, and what will you change?
