Lockdown Procedures

Understanding when and how to initiate a lockdown, and mastering the immediate actions that keep students hidden, silent, and safe until trained responders arrive.

Learning Objective

To provide participants with a thorough understanding of lockdown procedures — from the reasoning behind them to the precise sequence of actions required — so that every response becomes automatic, decisive, and completed within thirty seconds.

After completing this lesson, participants will be able to:

  1. Identify the situations that require a lockdown and distinguish them from scenarios requiring evacuation or other protective actions
  2. Understand why speed of concealment — not strength of barricade — is the primary survival factor, and how attacker behaviour under stress confirms this
  3. Execute the full seven-step immediate response sequence: command, lock, darken, move, silence, count, communicate — within thirty seconds
  4. Apply correct decision-making for locked-out students, bathroom safety, injured persons outside the secured room, and the rare circumstances that justify relocation from a secured position

Lesson 19: Lockdown Procedures

In the previous lesson, you explored the risks and challenges that schools face. Some of those risks are slow-building — patterns that develop over weeks, months, years. This lesson is about the ones that arrive in seconds.

A lockdown is initiated when an immediate threat exists inside or is approaching the school. It is one of the most critical decisions school leadership will ever make — and one of the most critical responses a teacher will ever carry out.

The instinct most people have about lockdowns is wrong. They imagine heavy barricades. Furniture piled against doors. Fortified classrooms. But the research tells a different story. The single most important factor in surviving a violent intrusion is not how strong the barrier is. It is how fast people disappear from sight.

Attackers experience extreme tunnel vision. They react to what is visible — movement, light, sound. They do not conduct room-by-room searches. A closed door with darkness behind it signals "empty" and "not worth the effort." A student crouched behind a desk, even imperfectly hidden, is vastly safer than one still visible in a corridor.

This means speed defeats perfection. Every time.

That principle will challenge you. Because when fear takes hold, the instinct is to do more — to find the perfect hiding place, to build the strongest barricade, to wait until everything is exactly right. But every second spent refining is a second of exposure. The correct response is immediate, simple, and practised until it is automatic.

This lesson will take you through every element of a lockdown — step by step. You will learn the seven actions a teacher must complete within thirty seconds. You will confront the hardest scenarios: students locked outside, injured persons in the corridor, the agonising decision of whether to open a secured door. You will learn when staying is safer than moving — and the rare, objective conditions under which relocation becomes necessary.

Some of what you encounter here will be difficult. The idea that you may need to keep a door locked while a student is outside it. The reality that helping others is only possible after those in your care are safe. The principle that children must never be instructed to physically confront an attacker, no matter what they may have seen in films or heard in other programmes.

These are not comfortable truths. But they are the truths that save lives.

You do not need to become a tactical expert. But you do need the confidence that comes from knowing exactly what to do, in what order, without hesitation. That confidence is built through understanding first and practice second.

This lesson provides the understanding. The drills that follow will build the muscle memory.

Fact Sheet

Lockdown Procedures for Schools — Detailed Quick Reference for School Staff

A lockdown is initiated when an immediate threat exists inside or is approaching the school grounds. Understanding when to initiate a lockdown — rather than evacuation or another measure — is one of the most critical decisions school leadership must make.

Core Principle: Speed Over Perfection. The most important factor for surviving a violent intrusion is not how strong the barricade is, but how fast people disappear from sight. Every second counts. Quick movement to cover behind any barrier is better than delayed movement behind a perfect barrier.
1

Why Speed Is Decisive — Attacker Behaviour

Perpetrators in violent intrusion situations typically experience severe tunnel vision during the event. They focus on what is immediately visible and moving directly in front of them. They do not normally conduct systematic room-by-room searches. Instead, they react to visual cues — movement, light, sound — and attack what most obviously presents itself.

A closed classroom door with lights off provides significant protection — if achieved quickly. The door itself is a psychological and physical barrier that attackers often choose not to force, especially if easier targets exist. Darkness creates uncertainty about whether anyone is there. The closed door signals "clear," "secured," "no opportunity."

Students hiding under desks, in dark corners, or behind furniture are at considerably less risk than those still visible in corridors — even if the hiding place is not perfect.

2

When Is a Lockdown Initiated?

  • Armed or violent person on school grounds
  • Credible and specific threat of violence directed at the school
  • Dangerous person at large in the school's immediate vicinity
  • Police order to secure the building
  • Other immediate danger requiring students and staff to remain indoors
The guiding message is simple and direct: "Stay inside — lock the door." The simplicity is intentional. Complex instructions are rarely followed correctly under the stress and fear of a real emergency.
3

The Teacher's Role

Teachers are the first line of protection during a violent intrusion. Their actions during the first seconds and minutes — swift, calm, deliberate — determine whether students remain invisible and safe until trained resources arrive.

The moment a lockdown alarm is received — via digital message, verbal code, or other means — the teacher must act immediately and decisively. There is no time for discussion, information gathering, or questions. The response must be automatic and immediate.

Teachers who waste precious seconds discussing exactly where students should hide, moving furniture to perfect positions, or searching for "better" alternatives lose time when speed matters most. The instruction to students must be immediate and simple: "Down and out of sight — now." Refinement can happen in the seconds after everyone is initially hidden, but the first quick movement away from the line of sight is the most critical action.
4

Teacher's Immediate Actions — Within 30 Seconds

All these actions should occur within thirty seconds. This is ambitious but achievable with practice and clear routines. The speed of this initial response is the primary safety factor.

Step 1 — COMMAND: Give a clear order in a firm, authoritative voice — a phrase students already recognise from regular drills. Signal that the instruction requires immediate compliance without questioning or delay.
Step 2 — LOCK: Lock the classroom door immediately. If the door has a glass panel or sight panel — cover it immediately with a pre-fitted covering.
Step 3 — LIGHTS OFF: Immediately after securing the door, turn off the lights. Darkness makes it harder to see in, creates uncertainty about whether the room is occupied, and makes the room less attractive as a target.
Step 4 — MOVE STUDENTS: Lead students away from doors and windows. Movement should be quick but without panic. Students move to the far side of the room, crouch or sit on the floor, and position themselves behind or under furniture if possible. The goal is to break the line of sight from corridor or exterior windows.
Step 5 — TOTAL SILENCE: Establish total silence — equally critical as the physical measures. A silent classroom provides no acoustic clues that the room is occupied. Use hand signals or very quiet commands.
Step 6 — COUNT: Do a quick visual count of students. Not time for roll calls or lists, but a quick overview: approximately how many are inside? Mentally note missing students — those who went to the bathroom, to the school nurse, or are absent.
Step 7 — COMMUNICATE: Send a short text message to school leadership using a predetermined short-format template. The message should only contain essential information: that the room is secured and a count of secured students. Send as SMS, not a call — to maintain silence and not occupy lines that emergency services may need.
5

Furniture and Barricades

If furniture is to be moved, the teacher does it themselves or uses only light objects that students can safely handle. The arrangement should delay but not permanently block a door, so that rescue personnel can enter if needed.

IMPORTANT: Never create a fire hazard. Never block all exits. Never leave locked-in persons without a retreat route. Never use methods requiring heavy lifting by children. All physical security techniques should be taught in staff training in cooperation with police and rescue services.
6

Locked-Out Students — One of the Hardest Scenarios

One of the hardest scenarios for teachers is discovering that students are outside the classroom when the lockdown begins — e.g. in the bathroom, at lockers, or between lessons. The teacher must quickly determine if and how these students can be brought in, while weighing the safety of those already secured inside.

The default position must be risk-averse: do not open the door for an unverified person. This may feel harsh, but opening a secured door creates vulnerability for everyone inside. The teacher cannot be certain that the person knocking is truly a student and not a threat trying to gain entry.

If students are known to be outside:

  • Note names and last known location for each locked-out student
  • Report to school leadership directly via the communication system
  • Do not open the door without verification according to predetermined procedures

Alternatives for locked-out students:

  • Seek the nearest safe room — instruct students to try the nearest doors, identify themselves clearly, and request entry
  • Bathroom as emergency solution — if no rooms admit them, students should find the nearest bathroom, lock the stall, and stay there
The teacher must resist the impulse to leave the secured group to retrieve locked-out students. That impulse is strong — every teacher's instinct is to protect everyone — but leaving a secured classroom exposes both the teacher and the group to greater risk. Rescue of locked-out students should be organised by school leadership.
7

Bathroom Safety

The Student's Rule: Are you in the bathroom when an alarm sounds? LOCK AND STAY. Do not try to return to the classroom. Bathrooms provide immediate lockable protection. Trying to move through corridors to reach a classroom increases exposure significantly.
8

Relocation from Secured Position

The general principle during lockdown is to stay in the secured space. Movement creates exposure and risk.

Relocation should occur ONLY when:

  • Fire makes the location untenable
  • Immediate proximity to threat that has breached the space
  • Structural damage compromising safety of the space
  • Explicit instructions from emergency services that evacuation is required

If relocation becomes necessary, it should be done in short, controlled steps — a few metres at a time toward documented safer locations.

9

Recommended Classroom Safety Equipment

  • Approved door stops or quick-locks that can be placed and removed easily
  • Coverings for door sight panels, pre-fitted to the building's doors
  • Internal locking mechanisms that can be locked without a key from inside
  • Emergency kit within arm's reach of the door
Emergency kit should contain: class list, charged phone, first aid kit, torch, and pre-folded window coverings.
10

Physical Confrontation — Children Must Never Be Instructed to Intervene

Encouraging untrained children to physically intervene against a violent attacker increases risk significantly. Research and experience clearly show why:

  • Insufficient physical capacity — children cannot reliably overpower an adult attacker, especially one who is armed
  • Increased exposure — such an attempt requires children to expose themselves to direct danger when they could otherwise have remained hidden and safe
  • Psychological burden — instructing children to prepare to attack another human being has deep and lasting psychological impact
Physical confrontation with an attacker must be a last resort, carried out only by trained adults, within the bounds of the law, and must not be part of students' training.

Summary

"Stay inside — lock the door." All actions — command, lock door, lights off, move students, establish silence, count, and communicate — should occur within thirty seconds. This is ambitious but achievable with practice and clear routines.

Step 5 of 6

Reflection

Take a moment to reflect on what you have learned and how it applies to your professional context.

Consideration

Think about the statement: "The risks that go unpractised are the risks that go unmanaged." What does this mean for how your school currently prepares for a lockdown — not in policy documents, but in actual, repeated practice?

Think about whether your response in the first thirty seconds of a real lockdown would be automatic or hesitant — and consider what is creating that gap.

Self-Reflection Questions

1

If a lockdown were initiated at your school right now — this moment — could you complete all seven steps within thirty seconds? Be honest with yourself about which steps you would hesitate on, and why.

2

Have you ever practised a lockdown with your students in realistic conditions — lights off, total silence, full duration? If not, what has prevented it, and what message does that send about your school's readiness?

3

Think about the principle that speed of concealment matters more than strength of barricade. Does this challenge how you previously understood lockdown procedures? How would this shift in thinking change what you do in the first five seconds?

4

Consider the locked-out student scenario. A student is knocking on your secured door. You cannot verify who it is. Could you keep that door closed? What emotional and ethical preparation would you need to make that decision under pressure — and have you had that conversation with yourself or your colleagues?

5

Do you know, right now, which students are likely to be outside your classroom at any given time — on bathroom breaks, at lockers, with the school nurse? How would you communicate their names and last known locations to school leadership during a lockdown?

6

Think about the bathroom rule: "Lock and stay." Have your students actually been taught this? Would a student in year seven, alone in a bathroom, know what to do if an alarm sounded — or would they try to run back to the classroom?

7

How confident are you that every member of staff at your school — not just teachers, but substitutes, support staff, canteen workers, caretakers — knows the lockdown procedure and could execute it independently? Rate your school's collective preparedness on a scale of 1-10, and identify the weakest link.

8

Consider the instruction that children must never be encouraged to physically confront an attacker. Is this clearly understood at your school, or is there ambiguity about what students should do if directly confronted? What has been communicated to students, and is it consistent?

9

Does your classroom have the recommended equipment — a quick-lock or door stop, a sight-panel cover, an emergency kit within arm's reach of the door? If not, what would it take to have these in place by the end of this term?

10

Name three specific actions you will take within the next two weeks to improve your personal readiness and your classroom's physical preparedness for a lockdown. Be concrete — not "I will think about it," but "I will do this, by this date."
