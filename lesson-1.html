  2. Define the role that criminology and its tools have in understanding and addressing adolescent criminality.
  3. Know the elements of the crime triangle and understand how it relates to school security.

Lesson 1: Security Principles

School security is not just about locks, cameras, or emergency plans. It is a system — built on people, communication, routines, and leadership working together.

When security is understood only as technology or policy, gaps appear. Threats go unnoticed. Responses become reactive instead of preventive. And the people closest to the problem — teachers, staff, students — are left without the tools or confidence to act.

Effective security starts with understanding. Understanding the difference between security and safety. Understanding how threats develop. Understanding what makes a school vulnerable — and what makes it resilient.

You don't need a background in law enforcement or crisis management to contribute to a safer school. You need awareness, structure, and a shared language for talking about risk.

This lesson gives you that foundation. It introduces the core concepts, models, and frameworks that underpin systematic security work — from the Crime Triangle to the 5D+1R model, from threat assessment to building a culture of shared responsibility.

Security is not one person's job. It is everyone's mindset.

Small steps — learning the basics, recognising patterns, knowing who to talk to — can transform how your school prevents, responds to, and recovers from incidents.

You don't need to be a security expert to make your school safer. You just need to understand how security works — and where you fit in.

Fact Sheet: Security Fundamentals

This fact sheet provides foundational knowledge for understanding security in educational environments, with a particular focus on Swedish schools.

1

Security vs Safety — The Fundamental Difference

Core Principle: Intent is the decisive difference between security and safety.

Security (Intentional Threats)

Protection against intentional, malicious threats such as crime, violence, or unauthorised intrusion.

Safety (Unintentional Risks)

An interdisciplinary field focused on minimising unintentional harm to people's health, safety, and the physical environment.

"Security is about people protecting people from people!"

Security threats: theft, vandalism, cyberattacks, intrusion, violence

Safety hazards: wet floors, faulty wiring, chemical spills, fire risks, equipment failures

2

The Seven Elements of Security Systems

Security functions as a multidisciplinary, multi-layered system managed through continuous communication and collaboration. The seven fundamental elements are:

  • Trained and motivated people — the human factor at the centre of security
  • Communication — trust-based information sharing and coordination
  • Routines — clear roles, structured guidance, and defined processes
  • Information and intelligence — purposeful processing of security-relevant data
  • Physical elements — barriers, structures, and design features
  • Technology — cameras, sensors, access controls, and communication systems
  • Leadership and decision-making — clear command structure and coordination
Integration Principle: All elements interact as overlapping protective layers to fulfil the core functions known as 5D+1R.
3

The 5D+1R Framework

The core functions that security systems must provide:

  • Deter — discourage potential threats
  • Detect — identify problems at an early stage
  • Deny — prevent unauthorised access
  • Delay — slow the progression of a threat
  • Defend — actively respond to threats
  • Recover — return to normal operations after incidents

These functions are applied across three main phases: prevention, response, and recovery.

4

The Crime Triangle Model

Fundamental Principle: All three elements must be present for a crime to occur.

The three components are:

  • Motive — the psychological or emotional driving force ("why")
  • Opportunity — the circumstances that make crime possible ("when/where")
  • Means — the resources, tools, or capabilities needed ("how/with what")

Prevention strategy: Remove a single element to significantly reduce the risk of crime.

Application in schools: "Your vulnerability = their opportunity."

5

"See, Say, Act" — Security Culture

A framework for building security awareness where everyone participates:

  • SEE: Develop awareness to recognise warning signs and threats
  • SAY: Create safe, accessible reporting channels without fear of judgement
  • ACT: Ensure consistent, appropriate responses to reported issues
Goal: Transform security from a set of rules into a shared mindset and responsibility.
6

Swedish Legal Framework

Key legislation for school security:

  • The Education Act (2010:800): Requires a safe, supportive learning environment and promotes democratic values
  • The Discrimination Act (2008:567): Prevents discrimination and requires active preventive measures
  • The Work Environment Act: Requires risk assessments and preventive safety measures
Recent changes (2025): Expanded powers for searches, mandatory plans for unauthorised access, and stronger collaboration with the justice system.
7

Training and Professional Development

Current challenge: Many Swedish schools rely on inadequate annual four-hour emergency training.

Need for systematic, role-based training that:

  • Addresses multifaceted and evolving risks
  • Offers continuous professional development
  • Includes scenario-based exercises
  • Builds security competence as part of professional identity

Conclusion

Security in schools requires a comprehensive, systematic approach that goes beyond installing technology or producing policy documents. It requires a culture where trained, motivated people collaborate through effective communication, clear routines, and coordinated leadership to create safe and resilient learning environments.

The combination of preventive and response capabilities, together with an understanding of both intentional threats and how they can be addressed, forms the foundation for effective school security management.

In a Swedish context, specific legal requirements and cultural factors apply — particularly the low power distance and consensus-oriented decision-making processes — which can strengthen security when understood and applied correctly.
