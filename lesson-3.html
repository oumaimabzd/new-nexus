The Legal Context

Introducing the legal framework that governs school security and explaining why understanding the law is essential for effective action.

Learning Objective

To establish a clear understanding of the legal rights, responsibilities, and obligations that shape how schools prevent, respond to, and manage security-related incidents.

After completing this lesson, participants will be able to:

  1. Understand the national strategies that position schools as key actors in Sweden's broader security and crime prevention framework
  2. Identify the key laws and regulations — including Chapter 6a of the Education Act — that define what schools can and must do to maintain safety
  3. Know their legal obligations regarding reporting, duty of care, physical intervention, and data protection

Lesson 3: The Legal Context

Every action you take — or fail to take — in a school security situation has a legal dimension. Whether you're confiscating a prohibited item, reporting a concern to social services, or physically intervening to protect a student, the law defines both your authority and your limits.

Many school staff feel uncertain about what they're allowed to do. That uncertainty leads to hesitation. And in security, hesitation creates risk — not just for students, but for the staff members themselves.

The truth is, Swedish law gives schools significant powers to act. Since 1 July 2025, Chapter 6a of the Education Act has granted principals the authority to control access, search bags, and confiscate dangerous items. But with that authority comes responsibility — to act proportionally, to document decisions, and to respect the rights of every child.

This isn't just about one chapter in one law. School security sits at the intersection of the Education Act, the Work Environment Act, the Social Services Act, the Discrimination Act, GDPR, and the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child. It is shaped by three national strategies that, for the first time, explicitly define schools as frontline actors in preventing extremism, organised crime, and youth recruitment into criminal networks.

Understanding this legal landscape is not optional. It is the foundation that gives your actions legitimacy and protects you professionally.

You don't need to be a lawyer to navigate these rules. You need to understand the principles behind them — duty of care, proportionality, mandatory reporting, and the best interests of the child. These principles don't just tell you what to do. They tell you why you're doing it.

When staff understand the legal context, something shifts. Confidence replaces hesitation. Decisions become clearer. And the gap between knowing something is wrong and knowing how to act on it disappears.

The law is not a barrier to keeping schools safe. It is the framework that makes it possible.

Fact Sheet

The Legal Context — Official Framework for School Security and Crime Prevention

Core Message: School security in Sweden operates within a comprehensive legal framework that empowers schools to act decisively while protecting student rights. This legal structure integrates national security strategies with education legislation, work environment regulations, and data protection regulations to create a multi-layered system for safety in the school environment.
1

Sweden's Three-Part National Security Strategy

1. National Strategy Against Violent Extremism and Terrorism (17 January 2024)
  • Prevent: Systematic work against radicalisation
  • Avert: Intelligence-based interventions against immediate threats
  • Protect: Strengthened protection for infrastructure and societal resilience
  • Manage: Prepared measures for incidents and recovery
2. "Resistance and Decisive Action" Against Organised Crime (1 February 2024)
First strategy to define organised crime as a national threat.
  1. Stop criminal career paths
  2. Limit access to illegal weapons
  3. Break the criminal economy
  4. Strengthen societal resilience
  5. Improve identification and information sharing
3. "Barriers Against Crime" (21 March 2024)
Schools become the first line of defence — responsible for identifying vulnerable students, cooperating with authorities, and applying evidence-based measures before legal sanctions are required.
2

Chapter 6a of the Education Act — The Decisive Reform

Background: SOU 2024:17 identified gaps → Proposition 2024/25:160 (16 April 2025) → Adopted 4 June 2025 → In force 1 July 2025

Key Provisions:

§3 — Preparedness for serious violent situations: Schools shall develop action plans and maintain constant readiness.
§4 — Access control: Restrict unauthorised access. Parents do NOT have automatic right of entry — the principal assesses based on student interest and safety.
§5 — Bag searches: The principal may authorise bag searches when there is risk of disorder, harassment, crime, or security threats. Must be reasonable and proportionate.
§§7-8 — Confiscation: Items returned by end of day, maximum 4 days (guardians notified). Weapons/narcotics reported to police immediately.
Significance: One of the most comprehensive legal changes in Swedish school security. Makes schools statutory actors in national security work.
3

Education Act (2010:800) — Other Relevant Parts

  • Ch. 1 §5: Actively promote democratic values and counteract threatening ideologies
  • Ch. 1 §10: All children shall be provided with safety in a secure environment
  • Ch. 6: Zero tolerance for degrading treatment (verbal, physical, psychological). Annual reviews and action plans required. Whistleblowers protected
  • Ch. 10 §2: Focus on personal and social development as protective factors
4

Other Key Legislation

Discrimination Act (2008:567): Prohibits discrimination. Schools must actively work against discriminatory treatment.
Social Services Act (2001:453): Ch. 5 §1: Ensure safe environments. School staff have a statutory duty to report when there is suspicion that a child is being harmed.
Work Environment Act & AFS 2023:2 (from 1 Jan 2025): Requires risk assessments for threats and violence, alarm systems, crisis support, first aid, and restrictions on lone working.
Weapons Act (1996:67): Prohibits weapons in public spaces, including schools. Chapter 6a gives principals the right to search, confiscate, and report.
Proposition 2024/25:141 (from 2 July 2025): New offence: insulting a public official, including teachers. Penalty: fines or up to 6 months imprisonment.
Lgr22 (National Curriculum): Democratic values, critical thinking, and inclusive teaching. A foundational security strategy.
UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (from 1 Jan 2020): Full legal force in Swedish law. The best interests of the child shall guide all decisions.
5

GDPR and Data Protection

Example (2019): A Swedish school was fined 200,000 SEK for facial recognition without valid legal basis or impact assessment.
  • Legal basis required (consent invalid for minors)
  • DPIA for high-risk processing
  • Purpose limitation and data minimisation
  • Privacy by design and transparency
6

Public Access and Secrecy Act (2009:400)

  • Ch. 18 §13: Protects risk assessments if disclosure could hinder crime prevention work
  • Ch. 38: Secrecy for civil defence and crisis planning materials
  • Only sensitive content may be classified
  • Secrecy is lifted when the need ceases
7

Crimes and Offences in the School Context

School-Related Crimes:

  • Truancy (violation of compulsory schooling, ages 6-16)
  • Assault and threats (Criminal Code)
  • Sexual offences: all acts involving children under 15 are punishable regardless of consent
  • Child pornography offences (including fictional images such as manga)
  • Cyber extortion and "sextortion"
  • Weapons possession: mandatory reporting and confiscation
  • Degrading treatment and bullying: preventive obligation

Narcotics and Alcohol:

  • Possession, use, and distribution prohibited (Narcotics Penalties Act 1968:64)
  • Sale to minors illegal; staff shall report

Recruitment Offences:

Ch. 16 §15a (from 1 July 2023): Criminalises recruitment of persons under 18 to criminal activity. Penalty: up to 4 years imprisonment.
Terrorist offences (from 1 June 2023): Membership, support, and recruitment to terrorist organisations criminalised — including for minors.
8

Duty of Care

Legal basis: Education Act (2010:800) — proactive responsibility for safety during all operational hours.

Scope:

Applies during lessons, breaks, excursions, transport, and after-school activities. Requires planning, preparedness, and daily vigilance.

Staff Responsibilities:

  • Actively monitor and intervene in unsafe situations
  • Act within proportionality and respect for student rights
  • No disciplinary authority outside school activities

Consequences of Failure:

  • Warnings, injunctions, or financial sanctions
  • Possible revocation of school licence
  • Civil or criminal liability
9

Physical Intervention (Education Act 5:6)

Permitted only when:
  • Reasonable and necessary to maintain order or prevent violence/harassment
  • The measure is proportionate
  • It is not used as punishment
  • Non-physical alternatives have been tried
Legal support: Duty of care, necessity and self-defence provisions (Criminal Code 24:1 and 24:4). The student's dignity must always be preserved.
10

Reporting Obligations

To the police (Ch. 6a §10): The principal is obliged to report crimes connected to school activities, unless there are special reasons not to.
To social services (Social Services Act Ch. 14 §1): Mandatory obligation when a child is suspected of being harmed — regardless of whether the event occurred within or outside school. The threshold is low: suspicion is sufficient.
Important: The staff's task is to report, not to investigate.
11

Proposed Changes (Ongoing)

  • Preventive surveillance of children under 15 suspected of gang connections (proposal April 2025)
  • Possible lowering of the age of criminal responsibility to 14-13 years
  • Abolished sentencing discount for 18-20 year olds (introduced January 2022)
  • Increased compulsory care and collaborative interventions for young people in serious criminality

Practical Summary — Schools' Responsibilities

  1. Integrate safety and study peace into the school's core values
  2. Build crime prevention capacity through risk analyses
  3. Protect staff through work environment measures
  4. Give staff the mandate to act within clear frameworks
  5. Apply rules proportionately and transparently
  6. Ensure data protection in accordance with GDPR
  7. Conduct self-assessments and exercise due diligence
  8. Cooperate with social services, police, and guardians

Step 5 of 6

Reflection & Additional Information

Take a moment to reflect on what you have learned and how it applies to your professional context.

Consideration

Think about the statement: "Uncertainty about the law doesn't remove your responsibility — it only delays your response." What does this mean for how your school prepares staff to act within their legal authority?

Think about a recent situation at your school where a staff member hesitated to act — and consider whether that hesitation was caused by a genuine legal limitation or by a lack of understanding of what the law actually permits.

Self-Reflection Questions

1

Before this lesson, how well did you understand the powers granted to schools under Chapter 6a of the Education Act — such as bag searches, access control, and confiscation? Has your school communicated these powers clearly to all staff, or are most people unaware of what they are now legally authorised to do?

2

Think about your school's mandatory reporting obligations — both to social services and to the police. Can you honestly say that every member of staff knows when they are legally required to report, what the threshold is, and how to do it? If not, what is the consequence of that gap?

3

Consider the principle of proportionality. Can you recall a situation at your school where a response to a security incident may have been either too heavy-handed or too passive? How would a clearer understanding of legal proportionality have changed the outcome?

4

Sweden's national strategies now position schools as frontline actors in preventing extremism, organised crime, and youth recruitment. Does your school acknowledge this role — or is it still treated as someone else's problem? What would need to change for your school to take this responsibility seriously?

5

How confident are you that your school handles student data in full compliance with GDPR — particularly when it comes to incident reports, security monitoring, or sharing information with police and social services? Rate your confidence on a scale of 1-10, and identify where the greatest uncertainty lies.

6

Think about duty of care and physical intervention. If a violent incident occurred in your corridor tomorrow, would you know exactly what you are legally permitted to do — and where the line is? Would your colleagues? If the answer is no, that is not a personal failing — it is an organisational one.

7

The law protects staff who act in good faith within their authority, but it also holds schools accountable for failing to act at all. Which risk is greater at your school right now — overstepping legal boundaries, or failing to use the authority you already have?

8

Name three specific actions you will take within the next month to strengthen your understanding of your legal responsibilities — and ensure that at least one of those actions involves sharing that knowledge with a colleague who may need it.

Summary of the Evolution
From 2020 to 2025, Swedish legislation has undergone a dramatic transformation that positions schools as frontline institutions within national security. Previously, schools could handle bullying and order, but lacked explicit mandate for broader security threats. With the three national strategies (extremism, organised crime, and barriers against crime) and the introduction of Chapter 6a, schools have received: a clear legal basis for security measures, authority to control, confiscate, and report, an obligation to cooperate with police and social services, strengthened protection for staff, and integration into the national crime prevention architecture.
