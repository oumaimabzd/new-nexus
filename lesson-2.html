Learning Objective

To provide participants with a comprehensive understanding of the risk landscape facing schools, including the interconnected nature of threats and the factors that make young people both vulnerable and, in some cases, capable of causing harm.

After completing this lesson, participants will be able to:

  1. Identify the principal categories of risk present in school environments — from violence and gang recruitment to digital threats and substance abuse.
  2. Understand the age-crime curve and explain why adolescence represents a critical window for both risk and intervention.
  3. Recognise how risks are interconnected rather than isolated, and why addressing root causes matters more than treating symptoms.
  4. Describe the legal and policy context that shapes how schools are expected to respond to these challenges.

Lesson 2: Risks and Challenges in Schools

In Lesson 1, you learned that security is a system — and that everyone has a role in making it work. But a system is only as strong as its understanding of what it is designed to protect against.

This lesson is about the reality of what schools face.

Not in theory. Not in headlines. In classrooms, corridors, schoolyards, and group chats — every day.

The risks are varied. Gang networks recruiting children as young as twelve. Students self-harming in silence. Sexual violence that goes unreported because no one knows how to recognise or respond to it. Online fraud and manipulation targeting teenagers who have never been taught to spot it. Substance use that starts with a vape and ends with dependency.

These are not separate problems with separate solutions. They are connected. Bullying feeds dehumanisation. Dehumanisation enables violence. Gang presence drives drug distribution. Digital harassment spills into physical confrontation. A student who steals may be a student who is being victimised. A student who lashes out may be a student living with violence at home.

If you try to address one risk in isolation, you will miss the pattern. And patterns are where prevention lives.

This lesson will take you through each major risk category — not to alarm you, but to equip you. You will see the data. You will understand the mechanisms. And you will begin to see how the security principles from Lesson 1 apply directly to real-world threats.

Some of what you encounter here will be uncomfortable. That is the point. The risks that go unspoken are the risks that go unmanaged.

You do not need to become an expert in criminology, psychology, or child protection. But you do need to know what to look for, why it matters, and what to do when you see it.

Awareness is the first line of defence. And it starts here.

Fact Sheet

School Security — Risks and Challenges

Core Message: Schools face a complex risk landscape where security threats are interconnected rather than isolated. Despite the education system encompassing nearly two million students across approximately 13,000 institutions, systematic and comprehensive security planning remains lacking compared to other sectors.
1

Key Concepts and Frameworks

The Age-Crime Curve

Criminal behaviour peaks during mid-to-late adolescence (15-19 years), driven by brain development patterns. The socio-emotional system (reward and sensation seeking) matures faster than the prefrontal cortex (impulse control), creating a vulnerability window.

Context: Nearly half of all men have been convicted of a crime by age 64, with a peak between ages 17-19. This underscores the crucial role of schools as intervention points.

Interconnection of Risks

Most security threats in school environments share common causes and often lead to chain reactions:

  • Gang presence leads to drug use and theft
  • Digital harassment leads to physical bullying
  • Bullying leads to targeted violence

Conclusion: Isolated risk management treats symptoms, not root causes.

2

Principal Risk Categories

Gang Criminality and Violent Crime

Criminal networks actively recruit minors. +30% suspects under 18 in gang-related shootings (2022-2023). In 2024: 93 children under 15 suspected of murder plots — three times more than the year before.

Dehumanisation as a Foundation for Violence

Viewing others as less than human enables moral disengagement. 46% of school attacks are motivated by prolonged bullying. Pattern: bullying, dehumanisation, loss of empathy, targeted attack.

Sexual Violence

Approximately 25% of cases involve perpetrators under 18. Globally: 1 in 8 girls and 1 in 11 boys are victimised before age 18. Cultural competence is critical for detection and reporting.

Self-Harm and Suicide

Suicide is the 3rd leading cause of death (ages 15-29). In Stockholm: 17% have self-harmed by age 18. Age of onset: 15.7 years. Girls face 3x higher risk of hospitalisation.

Property Crime and Theft

26% of ninth graders report theft. Bullying often hides behind these acts — the purpose is power and humiliation. Can lead to depression and increased risk of self-harm.

Digital Risks

Nearly 50% of 8-17 year olds deceived online (UK). American youth lost $210M in 2023 (+2500% since 2017). Sweden: +950% cybercrime (2006-2015), 30 billion SEK losses in 2021.

Domestic Violence

Since 2021, it is a criminal offence to expose children to domestic violence in all settings, including schools. Schools must detect signs and handle legal custody decisions.

Substance Abuse

26% of 15-year-olds have tried cigarettes, approximately 33% use snus, 30%+ have vaped. Gang presence worsens the situation through direct distribution and recruitment of dependent youth.

Other Critical Risks

  • Arson and Vandalism: 40% of school fires in Sweden are deliberately set
  • Aggression: 1 in 40 fifteen-year-olds subjected to serious violence; 5 out of 6 reports concern threats against staff
  • Viral Pranks: TikTok's "Chromebook Challenge" (fires) and "Blackout Challenge" (fatalities)

Legal and Policy Context

  • 2025 Legislative Changes: Expanded powers, mandatory security plans, strengthened police cooperation
  • Education Act Section 6: Zero tolerance for degrading treatment
  • New Criminal Classification (July 2025): "Insulting a public official" — fines or up to 6 months imprisonment
  • Socioeconomic Disparities: Schools in vulnerable areas are disproportionately affected

Prevention Philosophy

Effective school security is not primarily built on technology but on culture and shared responsibility. Security systems without trust can create fear rather than safety.

Real prevention arises through a culture where everyone — teachers, students, and parents — shares a common awareness, where warning signs are taken seriously, and where security is a natural part of everyday life without shame or stigma.

The human dimension — relationships, empathy, and vigilance — is and remains the strongest line of defence against all forms of risk in schools.

Self-Reflection Questions

1

Which of the risk categories covered in this lesson surprised you the most — and what does that tell you about potential blind spots at your school?

2

Can you identify a situation where interconnected risks were present at your school — for example, where bullying, social exclusion, or digital harassment may have been feeding a larger problem? What was done, and what could have been done differently?

3

Thinking about the age-crime curve and the vulnerability window of adolescence — are there students at your school who may be in that window right now, showing early warning signs that no one is addressing?

4

How confident are you that staff at your school would recognise the signs of self-harm, substance use, sexual violence, or gang recruitment? Rate your school's collective awareness on a scale of 1-10 — and identify what is holding that score back.

5

Think about the connection between what happens online and what happens in the school building. Is your school actively monitoring or addressing digital risks, or is that seen as something outside the school's responsibility?

6

Which risk do you believe your school is most exposed to right now — and what is the single biggest barrier to addressing it?

7

Name three specific actions you will take within the next month to improve your ability to recognise, report, or respond to one of the risks covered in this lesson.
